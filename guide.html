Culture Guide

The unwritten rules, decoded — so the only surprise of the night is how good the food is.

01 — House rules マナー5箇条

Five manners, that's all

🍻

Wait for kanpai

Don't drink until everyone has a glass and you toast together: "Kanpai!" First sip is communal.

🥢

Plates are for sharing

Dishes belong to the table. Take portions onto your small plate (torizara). Ordering "one karaage each" marks you as a rookie — order for the table.

🙋

Call staff — button or voice

Staff won't hover. Many shops have a call button (呼び出しボタン) on the table — press it once and someone appears. No button? Raise a hand and call "Sumimasen!" across the room. Both are normal, expected, and polite.

💴

No tipping

Not at the table, not in the bill, not "keep the change." Tipping causes confusion, and staff may chase you down the street to return your money.

🚬

Smoking varies

Some small izakaya still allow smoking indoors. If it matters to you, check before sitting — staff will ask or you can ask them.

TABLE BASICS — 卓上の作法

🧻 Oshibori — the wet towel

The rolled towel handed to you first (hot in winter, cool in summer) is for wiping your hands before eating. Then fold it neatly and keep it beside you — it doubles as your finger napkin for skewers and edamame all night. Wiping your face with it is the classic middle-aged-regular move: technically frowned upon, universally forgiven.

🥢 Waribashi — disposable chopsticks

Snap the joined chopsticks apart holding them horizontally. Don't rub them together to shave off splinters — it silently tells the shop "your chopsticks are cheap" and reads as rude. Between bites, rest them on the chopstick rest or laid across your small plate. Two hard taboos anywhere in Japan: never stand chopsticks upright in rice, and never pass food chopstick-to-chopstick — both mimic funeral rites.

02 — The famous mystery dish お通し擁護論

In defense of otoshi

Search "izakaya scam" and every result is about otoshi: the small dish you never ordered, added to the bill at ¥300–600 per person. Tourists feel tricked. Review sites fill with one-star anger. Let's fix that here.

Otoshi is a cover charge — with food attached. Izakaya let you occupy a table for three hours while ordering ¥350 edamame. The seat charge is what makes that economics work, and instead of a cold line item, the kitchen sends it out as a small dish: marinated vegetables, a bite of simmered fish, whatever the chef made that day.

Here's the reframe that changes everything: otoshi is the chef's handshake. It's the one plate the kitchen chose for you, and regulars judge a new izakaya by it. If the otoshi is good, the menu will be good. You're not being scammed — you're being introduced.

The practical version

Expect ¥300–600 per person on the bill. It is normal, legal, and near-universal. A tiny number of tourist-area shops abuse it with ¥1,500+ charges — if the shop has aggressive street touts pulling you in, walk away. A quiet shop full of locals never plays that game.

03 — When to go 時間帯別ガイド

The clock changes everything

17:00 – 18:30

The golden hour. Shops just opened, staff are relaxed, counter seats are free. The best time for solo travelers and first-timers. Some shops run happy-hour prices.

19:00 – 21:00

Peak. Loud, smoky, alive — the full experience, but on Friday and Saturday you may not get in without a reservation. Weekdays are much easier.

21:30 – close

The wind-down. Great atmosphere, but kitchens take "last order" 30–60 minutes before closing. If staff says 「ラストオーダーです」, it's your final chance to order — not a request to leave immediately.

One-person tip

Eating alone at an izakaya counter is completely normal in Japan — locals do it daily. Go at 17:30, sit at the counter, order toriaezu bīru and two skewers. You'll fit right in.

05 — Leaving well 締めの挨拶

Exit like a regular

One sentence on the way out earns you a chorus of thank-yous and a genuinely warm memory:

ごちそうさまでした
Gochisōsama deshita

"Thank you for the feast." Said when leaving. It's the highest compliment a full and happy customer can give.

美味しかったです!
Oishikatta desu!

"It was delicious!" Add this if you mean it. You will.
