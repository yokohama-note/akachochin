01 — What is an izakaya 居酒屋とは

Not a bar. Not a restaurant. Better.

An izakaya is Japan's after-work living room: a casual pub where small shared plates keep arriving while everyone drinks and talks for hours. There is no "main dish" and no rush — you order a little, eat, talk, order again.

Sushi and ramen are Japan's greatest hits. The izakaya is where you hear the whole album: fried chicken next to sashimi next to potato salad next to grilled fish, all on one table. If you have only one free evening in Japan, spend it under a red lantern.

How to spot one

Look for a red paper lantern (赤提灯 akachochin) outside and a short split curtain (のれん noren) over the door. If the curtain is up, they're open. Duck through it — that's the whole entrance ceremony.

02 — From door to bill 入店から会計まで

The whole evening in 7 steps

Nothing here is hard. It's just different — and now you know it in advance.

  1. Enter & state your number

    Staff will ask how many people. Fingers work perfectly.

    Staff says: 「何名様ですか?」 Nan-mei sama desu ka? — "How many?"

  2. Sit where you're guided

    Counter seats are great for 1–2 people and the best view of the kitchen. You may be asked to remove shoes for tatami seating.

  3. Order drinks first

    Drinks come first, food after — that's the rhythm. The classic opener:

    You say: 「とりあえずビール」 Toriaezu bīru — "Beer for now." The most Japanese sentence in existence.

  4. A small dish appears. You didn't order it.

    That's otoshi — a seat charge (¥300–600/person) served as a small appetizer. It is not a scam and not a free gift. Full story on the Guide page.

  5. Order food in waves

    Do it like a local: with the first drink, order 2–3 quick plates that arrive fast — edamame, potato salad, hiyayakko. Then re-order in rounds: over the night, locals call staff two or three more times, adding dishes as appetite dictates. Nothing is decided upfront — that's the point. Call staff with the table button, or by raising your hand and saying Sumimasen!

  6. Finish with a "shime"

    The closing carb: rice ball, ochazuke, or noodles. A beloved ritual.

  7. Ask for the bill

    You say: 「お会計お願いします」 O-kaikei onegaishimasu. Pay at the table or front register. No tipping — ever. Many small shops are cash only.
