DRINKS FIELD NOTES — 飲み物メモ

🍺 Beer: know the big four

Most izakaya pour only one or two brands on draft, and the lineup differs shop by shop — so you drink what the house drinks. Asahi Super Dry is sharp, dry, and crisp — built for fried food. Kirin Ichiban Shibori is smooth and gently rich, brewed only from the first press of the wort. Sapporo Black Label is balanced and quietly malty, the easygoing all-rounder. Yebisu (Sapporo's premium all-malt line) is rich and a notch pricier — a shop that pours Yebisu is quietly telling you it cares. Spotting which brand a shop carries is a small local sport.

🥃 Whisky? In Japan, order the highball

Japan's signature way to drink whisky is the highball — whisky and sparkling soda over ice. It's lighter than a neat pour, endlessly refillable, and cuts through fried and grilled food perfectly. When in doubt: 「ハイボールください」 Haibōru kudasai.

🚰 Water and tea are usually free

Still water (お水 o-mizu) and often hot tea (お茶 o-cha) are usually served free of charge — a small number of shops do charge, so treat free as the norm, not a guarantee. It's completely normal to ask for water as a chaser next to your drink, or after the meal. One real rule, though: every guest is expected to order at least one (paid) drink — sitting down and ordering only free water or tea is considered bad manners. Order your beer, sour, or oolong tea first; the water rides alongside. 「お水ください」 O-mizu kudasai / 「お茶ください」 O-cha kudasai.
