Ordering Tools

Point-and-show cards for the table. Set it up, hand over your phone, and you're understood — no Japanese needed.

01 — Point & order 指差し注文表

Order without reading a single menu

First rule: the shop's own menu is the best order sheet — point at it, and you're done. If there's an English menu, you barely need this section. These tools are for the other nights: handwritten-tanzaku, Japanese-only shops where pointing at the menu isn't possible.

EASIEST: LEAVE IT TO THE KITCHEN — おまかせで頼む

No menu reading at all. Set the number of plates, show the card, and let the kitchen choose — this is how regulars order, and kitchens love it.

🍽 One plate feeds the table

Izakaya dishes are shared — you do not order one edamame per person. For a group, one plate of each dish (two of the crowd-pleasers, at most) is the local way. Under-order on purpose: calling staff for round two later is exactly how it's done.

BACKUP: ITEM-BY-ITEM — 定番を一品ずつ

When you want specific classics by name: these dishes exist at almost every izakaya, so this slip is safe to show even without seeing the shop's menu. If a dish isn't available, staff will point at the "sold out" box on the card.

DRINKS — 飲み物

02 — Ask the pros おすすめを聞く

"Beef, around ¥1,000 — what do you recommend?"

The best dish in any izakaya is usually not on your radar — it's whatever the kitchen is proud of today. Pick a category and a budget, and we'll build the question in Japanese. Show it, and staff will point at the menu for you. And before you leave, ask about dessert — izakaya desserts (matcha ice cream, anmitsu, homemade pudding) are a quietly excellent finale.
